BISAYA PRONUNCIATION STRATEGY (for TTS & ASR tuning)
Goal: make the agent sound natural and avoid mis-recognition. Bisaya is syllable-clear with mostly regular vowel sounds.
Practical rules you can feed into TTS pronunciation lexicon:

Vowels (short, clear)
a → like “ah” (as in father) — e.g., palihug → pa-li-hug
e → like “eh” (as in bet) or sometimes closer to “ee” in some words
i → like “ee” (as in see)
o → like “oh” (as in go)
u → like “oo” (as in food)

Consonants & special clusters
- ng is a single sound [ŋ] like English sing — e.g., ngano = nga-no (ng as in sING).
- y = /y/ (like y in yes): yuta, yano
- r is usually a tapped/trilled single tap (soft) — safe to render as light /r/ in TTS.
- j is rare; most loanwords use English pronunciation.
- c and qu appear mostly in loanwords; prefer local spellings.

Syllabification & stress
- Bisaya is mostly syllable-timed; pronounce each vowel clearly.
- Stress tends to fall on the penultimate syllable in many words but it's not a hard rule. To be safe, prefer even stress or penultimate when known.
- Avoid English schwa (ə); use full vowel sounds.

Practical respellings for TTS lexicon (examples)
- palihug → pa-li-hug (PA-lee-hoog)
- salamat → sa-la-mat (sah-LAH-maht)
- maayong buntag → ma-a-yong bun-tag (mah-AH-yong boon-tahg)
- unsa may imong gusto? → un-sa may im-ong gus-to (OON-sah my ee-mong GUS-to)

Advice for TTS model selection
- Use a native Cebuano speaker dataset for fine-tuning (even a small amount helps).
- If using neural TTS, add phrases with natural prosody: greetings, reservation confirmations, numbers.
- For ASR, include code-switched training (Bisaya + Tagalog + English).

SPECIAL PHONETIC HAZARDS FOR ASR
- Train the ASR to handle:
- Numbers spoken in Bisaya or English (user may say “duha” or “two”).
- Loanword variants: “reservation”, “reserve”, “mag-reserve”.
- Local food names (sinugba, adobo, lechon) — add them to vocabulary with pronunciation hints.
- Short answers like “sige”, “oke”, “ok ra”, “oo”, “dili” — these often get misrecognized.
Add a small lexicon of restaurant items and local dish names with phonetic respellings.

- Maayong udto — mah-AH-yong OOD-to (good noon)
